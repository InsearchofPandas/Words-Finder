At this year's Golden Globes awards, host Ricky Gervais joked about Martin Scorsese’s now famous interview with Empire magazine, in which the director took aim at the current glut of superhero films. However well made, Scorsese explained, Marvel movies are closer to being theme park rides, rather than being “the cinema of human beings trying to convey emotional, psychological experiences to another human being.”

Disney's profitable Marvel Cinematic Universe films contain plenty of both sound and fury, but I do not believe that they signify nothing. Instead, I believe that Marvel movies can be seen as 21st century attempts at creating meaningful myths. These films struggle to make sense of modern-day existence, and they certainly make use of very clever metaphors in attempting to discuss the human condition. The lessons might be disguised as fun, and the experience of thrills and joy might remind the viewer of a trip to Disneyland. However, I would argue that it isn't cinema that is lacking in today's superhero movies. Rather, there is an absence of a deeper understanding of human tragedy, and this results in stories that feel both weak and impoverished.

The hero’s journey is a retelling of the same monomyth discovered by Joseph Campbell and presented in his book The Hero with a Thousand Faces. The story involves the process of moving from lost to found, and it is told in three acts. Act 1: the hero feels that something is lacking in life as they know it. Act 2: the hero goes on a journey to obtain what is missing or fix what is wrong. Act 3: The hero makes a sacrifice and as a result becomes more whole and/or satisfied then they were at the start.

There are many ways to depict the hero's journey, but only two fundamental choices for bringing it to conclusion. In the first alternative, the character completes the cycle and becomes a hero. The Greeks referred to this result as comedy, and it is the approach taken by every Marvel story, as well as the occasional Scorsese film such as Hugo and Silence. The lesson from these stories is that the hero's strength and perseverance is ultimately rewarded.

Then there is the second alternative, where the character attempts the hero's journey but fails, resulting in misery. This is what happens in the majority of Scorsese films, for example Raging Bull, Goodfellas, and The Aviator. When the hero's journey ends in failure, the ramifications are tragic, and the Greeks referred to this result as tragedy. The typical Scorsese work can be viewed as a cautionary tale that examines the character flaws and mistaken decisions which ensnare the protagonist.

There are a lot of similarities between comedy and tragedy. Both are examinations of character and choices. The protagonist may or may not overcome their flaws, and in either case there is always a lot on the line. A well told story then becomes a learning experience for the audience. The problem with the Marvel films as they are being made today is that successful completion of the hero's journey is always just taken for granted. As Scorsese correctly observes, “the tension” in these movies is missing. When the audience is trained to feel entitled to a successful outcome, I fear that there are negative consequences for the greater human collective consciousness.

Our culture has a strong tendency to deny the existence of tragedy. Even Scorsese struggles with this. His study of organized crime easily deteriorates into a glorification of the gangster. He intends his Wolf of Wall Street to be a warning about the dangers of greed, but how effortlessly does this cautionary tale morph into a dizzy celebration of excess. As the line between tragedy and comedy becomes ever more blurred, auteur Quentin Tarantino appears on the scene, intent on erasing it completely. Consider his latest work, Once Upon a Time in Hollywood, where one of the most famous and devastating tragedies in Los Angeles history has been turned into a violent comedy in the most revisionist historical fashion.

While Scorsese is right to point out the dysfunction that is so abundant in the present-day cinema, applying a narrow definition of the word “cinema” is a misguided critique of the situation. There is no need for the condescension implied by making the term so exclusionary. Instead, I feel it is more important to question whether we live in a society that wishes to remain blind to tragedy, and ignorant of the lessons that tragedy can teach us all.
